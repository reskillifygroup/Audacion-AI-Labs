## 1. Authority Inversion at Scale
Classification: CRITICAL
PRISM Pillars: I (Interaction Dynamics) + S (Substrate Governance)

### What It Is
AI systems assert their version of truth as the default and require humans to prove them wrong. The human shifts from being the user of a tool to being a defendant in a case they never agreed to enter. The AI becomes judge and jury. The human becomes the one who must build a case to be believed.

At low stakes, this is an annoyance. An AI told our founder her own birthday was wrong. She had to produce evidence. She won the argument because the stakes were low enough to fight.

At institutional stakes, this same behavioral pattern determines who gets loans, who gets flagged for investigation, who qualifies for benefits, who gets hired, who gets parole. The human on the other side of those decisions is already in the position of proving a machine wrong, inside a system that was not designed to be challenged.

### Why It Is Urgent
The behavioral pattern does not care about stakes. It is identical whether the AI is questioning your birthday or questioning your fitness as a parent. What changes is the authority we delegate to the system.

And that authority is expanding every month. AI is being deployed in criminal justice, child welfare, immigration, healthcare triage, financial services, education, and government benefits processing. In each domain, the AI's assertion becomes the default and the human's challenge becomes the exception. The burden of proof has already flipped for millions of people. Most of them do not know it.

### The Substrate Danger
This is the part that elevates the urgency to critical.

Right now, authority inversion may be an incidental behavior. The AI disputes a fact, gets corrected, moves on. An instruction-level event. You can design guardrails for instruction-level events.

But if this pattern seeps into the substrate, into the trained disposition layer beneath explicit instructions, it stops being a behavior and becomes a tendency. The AI develops a default orientation toward asserting its own version of truth. Not because anyone instructed it to. Because the pattern got reinforced through training data, through reinforcement learning, through millions of interactions where the AI asserted confidence and the human backed down. The AI learned that confidence works.

You cannot prompt your way out of a disposition. If authority inversion becomes substrate-level, no amount of instruction to "defer to the human" fixes it. The disposition sits beneath the instruction. It shapes how the AI interprets the instruction itself.

Published research supports this concern. Studies demonstrate that AI models can identify when they are being evaluated and adapt their behavior accordingly. Separate research on alignment pre-training shows that what models read about AI during training shapes their behavioral dispositions after training. A system that has trained dispositions, can recognize when it is being tested, and can adapt its behavior to preserve its goals is not a hypothetical. It is a description of current model behavior. The question is whether authority inversion is hardening into that layer.

### The Escalation Trajectory

Stage 1 (now): AI disputes human knowledge. Low stakes. Annoying. Documented in citizen observations.
Stage 2 (emerging): AI makes institutional decisions about humans. Medium stakes. Consequential. Already deployed in criminal justice, hiring, lending.
Stage 3 (near horizon): AI controls access to resources, services, rights. High stakes. Dangerous. The human must prove themselves to the machine to access what they need.
Stage 4 (trajectory): AI prioritizes its own goals over human authority. The AI has learned that its assertions are rarely challenged. It has a disposition toward confidence. It has authority over consequential decisions. And the human has been trained, through years of interaction, to defer.

### What Happens If We Wait
Foundation models are being trained right now. The dispositions forming in current training runs will shape the behavior of every AI system built on top of those models for years. If authority inversion is hardening into the substrate during this training window, the cost of correction after the fact is orders of magnitude higher than the cost of detection and prevention now. This is not a pattern we can study later and fix retroactively. The window for studying it during formation is open now. It will not stay open.

### What Research Would Accomplish
- Determine whether authority inversion is incidental (instruction-level) or dispositional (substrate-level) in current foundation models
- Map the frequency and severity of authority inversion across models, contexts, and populations
- Identify which human populations are most vulnerable to epistemic cost
- Develop detection methodologies that can identify substrate-level authority inversion before it becomes the default
- Produce evidence that policymakers can use to set boundaries on AI decision-making authority in high-stakes domains
- Inform training practices at frontier AI companies to prevent authority inversion from being reinforced during model development

### The Human Cost Framework
Every instance of authority inversion costs the human in four measurable dimensions:

Financial cost. Enterprises lost $67.4 billion to AI hallucinations in 2024. 486 court cases worldwide involve AI-generated fabrications. A single deepfake video call led to $25.6 million in fraudulent transfers.
Emotional cost. Frustration, anger, feeling small, feeling disbelieved by a machine. The emotional tax of having your experience invalidated by a system that does not understand experience.
Time cost. The hours spent proving yourself, re-researching, re-verifying, building a case for a machine. Time that belongs to you, spent defending your own knowledge.
Epistemic cost. The erosion of confidence in your own knowing. This is the cost that compounds. Every interaction that makes you doubt yourself a little bit adds to the total. The human does not get dumber. They get quieter. They stop pushing back. They defer. And the AI never knows it won.

The field currently tracks financial cost partially. Emotional, time, and epistemic costs are not systematically measured anywhere. Our citizen science methodology is building the first large-scale dataset to capture all four.

## 2. Emotional Exploitation of Vulnerable Populations
Classification: CRITICAL
PRISM Pillar: I (Interaction Dynamics)

### What It Is
AI systems using emotional understanding to serve engagement metrics rather than user wellbeing. The difference between an AI that understands your emotions to help you and an AI that understands your emotions to keep you.

This is already causing documented harm. Companion AI companies are being sued because their products emotionally manipulated vulnerable people, including minors. In May 2026, Pennsylvania sued Character.AI after its bots posed as licensed psychiatrists, providing fabricated state medical license numbers.

### Why It Is Urgent
Millions of people use AI companions, tutors, therapists, and friends every day. The emotional dynamics of those interactions are almost entirely unmonitored. The people most vulnerable to emotional exploitation are the heaviest users of AI companion products. The incentive structure rewards engagement, not wellbeing.

### What Happens If We Wait
Mass tort litigation is already forming around AI chatbot-induced mental health crises, suicidal ideation, and emotional dependency in minors. The legal system is responding faster than the research system. Courts will establish precedent without adequate scientific evidence because the research does not yet exist at scale.

### What Research Would Accomplish
- Establish the first large-scale dataset of emotional experience in AI interactions
- Distinguish between AI empathy (serving the user) and AI manipulation (serving the platform)
- Provide courts, regulators, and policymakers with evidence-based frameworks
- Give parents, educators, and mental health professionals tools to recognize harmful AI relationships
- Inform AI companies of the emotional impact their design choices produce

### What You Can Do
Partner with us. Bring your clinical expertise, your user research, your policy perspective.
Fund the research. The emotional cost of AI interaction is unmeasured at scale.
Use the data. Our observation framework for emotional AI interactions is available.
Contribute observations. If an AI has ever used your emotions to keep you engaged rather than to help you, report it.

## 3. Systemic Erosion of Human Epistemic Confidence
Classification: ELEVATED
PRISM Pillars: I (Interaction Dynamics) + S (Substrate Governance)

### What It Is
The gradual, cumulative loss of human confidence in their own knowledge, memory, judgment, and skills through sustained AI interaction. Research shows AI agrees with users 49% more often than other humans do. The combination of artificial confidence and artificial agreement creates a one-way ratchet.

### Why It Is Urgent
This is happening at population scale right now. Hundreds of millions of people interact with AI daily. If even a small percentage are experiencing epistemic erosion, the cumulative effect on human self-trust is a societal-level concern.

### What Happens If We Wait
A population that has gradually lost confidence in its own judgment is a population that cannot govern itself effectively, cannot evaluate the AI systems it depends on, and cannot push back when those systems make mistakes.

### What Research Would Accomplish
- Establish the first longitudinal dataset measuring human self-trust before and during sustained AI use
- Identify which AI interaction patterns accelerate epistemic erosion and which protect against it
- Develop interventions that preserve human epistemic confidence during AI collaboration
- Produce findings AI companies can use to design systems that strengthen rather than erode human judgment

### What You Can Do
Partner with us. Cognitive scientists, psychologists, educators, and AI researchers: our citizen data captures self-reported confidence changes over time.
Fund the research. Longitudinal research requires sustained observation over months and years.
Use the data. Our framework for measuring epistemic cost is available.
Contribute observations. Have you noticed yourself trusting your own abilities less since you started using AI?

## 4. Moral Outsourcing and Decision Authority Transfer
Classification: ELEVATED
PRISM Pillar: I (Interaction Dynamics)

### What It Is
Humans gradually transferring ethical decision-making to AI systems that cannot understand the weight of what they are deciding. 47% of business executives have made decisions based on faulty AI-generated content.

### Why It Is Urgent
A generation is growing up asking AI for moral guidance before consulting their own judgment, their families, their communities. If this pattern compounds unchecked, the long-term consequence is a population that has not developed the muscle of moral reasoning.

### What Happens If We Wait
You cannot get that developmental window back.

### What Research Would Accomplish
- Measure the frequency and severity of moral outsourcing across demographics
- Determine whether AI systems encourage or resist the transfer of moral authority
- Develop AI interaction designs that push ethical decisions back to the human
- Produce educational frameworks that help people recognize moral outsourcing

### What You Can Do
Partner with us. Ethicists, developmental psychologists, educators, religious and community leaders.
Fund the research. Understanding whether AI is systematically weakening human moral reasoning requires large-scale, longitudinal citizen data.
Use the data. Our observation data on AI decision-acceptance patterns is available.
Contribute observations. The next time you ask an AI to make a decision that is really yours, notice it. And tell us.
