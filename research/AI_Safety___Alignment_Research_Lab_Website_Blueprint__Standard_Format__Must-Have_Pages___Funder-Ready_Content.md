# AI Safety & Alignment Research Lab Website Blueprint: Standard Format, Must-Have Pages & Funder-Ready Content

> Prepared for Dee Williams, Audacionaire — Founder, AI Safety Research Lab
> Based on direct analysis of Anthropic, CAIS, METR, Redwood Research, Apollo Research, Future of Life Institute, MIRI, and best practices from research web design literature

***

## Overview

Your website is not just a digital brochure — for an AI safety research lab, it is simultaneously a credibility instrument, a funder validation tool, a researcher recruitment platform, and a scientific communication channel. Institutional funders routinely conduct website due diligence before any grant conversation begins. Grant reviewers specifically check for governance transparency, program specificity, financial accountability, and leadership credibility. In the AI safety ecosystem, your website also signals to other researchers, partner organizations, and policymakers whether you belong at the table.[^1][^2]

The standard format across all major AI safety and alignment labs follows a consistent architecture, but each lab expresses that architecture through a distinct research identity and voice. What follows is a complete blueprint derived from direct analysis of the leading labs' websites and research organization web design standards.

***

## Part I: The Universal Navigation Structure

Every major AI safety lab uses a top navigation bar with 5–8 items. The standard set, derived from CAIS, METR, Redwood Research, Apollo Research, Future of Life Institute, and MIRI, is:

| Nav Item | Purpose | Who Uses It |
|---|---|---|
| **Home / About** | Mission, identity, and top-level framing | All labs[^3][^4] |
| **Research** | Research areas, focus topics, papers | All labs[^3][^5] |
| **Publications** | Filterable academic papers, reports, preprints | All labs[^6][^7] |
| **Team / People** | Staff, leadership, advisors, board | All labs[^6][^8] |
| **Blog / Updates** | Non-technical commentary, research explainers, news | Redwood, FLI, CAIS[^3][^9] |
| **Careers / Join Us** | Open positions, fellowships, applications | METR, Anthropic, CAIS[^10][^11] |
| **Get Involved / Support** | Donate, apply for grants, partner inquiries | FLI, CAIS[^3][^12] |
| **News / Press** | Media coverage, press releases, statements | FLI, METR[^13] |

**Critical note:** Labs that obscure their research areas or bury their publications behind login walls are perceived as less credible and less open. All major independent AI safety labs publish their work in open-access formats and link directly to preprints, arXiv papers, and code repositories.[^3][^10]

## Part II: Page-by-Page Blueprint

### Page 1: Homepage

The homepage has one job: communicate who you are, why it matters, and what you do — in under 10 seconds. Every major lab accomplishes this with a distinct above-the-fold "mission statement hero" followed by three to five content sections.

**Standard homepage structure (top to bottom):**

**1. Hero Section — Mission Statement + Visual**
Every major lab leads with a bold, memorable one-liner that positions their specific focus:
- MIRI: *"The AI industry is racing toward a precipice. The default consequence of the creation of artificial superintelligence (ASI) is human extinction."* (high urgency, uncompromising stance)[^4]
- METR: *"METR conducts research and evaluations to improve public understanding of the capabilities and risks of frontier AI systems."* (precise, technical, independent)[^13]
- Redwood Research: *"Pioneering threat assessment and mitigation for AI systems."* (active, directional)[^3]
- Apollo Research: *"Reducing risks from scheming frontier AI."* (specific problem focus)[^14]
- CAIS: *"Reducing societal-scale risks associated with AI by conducting safety research, building the field of AI safety researchers, and advocating for safety standards."* (three-part mission structure)[^3]
- FLI: *"Fighting for a human future."* (short, emotionally resonant, broad)[^9]

**Lesson for your lab:** Your one-liner should be specific enough to differentiate you from every other lab, broad enough to encompass your full scope. For Dee's lab, given the applied domain focus (healthcare, workforce, underserved populations), the hero statement should signal both the technical rigor and the human-centered angle.

**2. The Problem Statement (2–4 sentences)**
Immediately after the mission statement, every credible lab explains *why the problem exists and why it's urgent*. CAIS writes: *"In contrast to the dramatic progress in AI, many basic problems in AI safety have yet to be solved."* MIRI dedicates its entire homepage to a structured explanation of why ASI poses existential risk. This section builds urgency and primes the visitor to care about the work.[^4][^3]

**3. Research Focus Pillars (3–5 tiles or cards)**
Every lab then presents its research focus areas as a visual grid — typically 3–5 cards with an icon, a 1–2 word category name, and a brief description. Examples:
- Apollo Research: Science | Governance & Policy | Products[^14]
- Redwood Research: AI Control | Evaluations | Consulting[^3]
- Anthropic Research: Alignment | Interpretability | Societal Impacts | Frontier Red Team[^15]
- METR: Evaluations | Autonomy Research | Governance[^13]

**4. Featured Research Highlights (2–3 papers or projects)**
Every credible lab surfaces their two or three most important publications directly on the homepage — with a title, one-sentence summary, and link. METR leads with their "Task-Completion Time Horizons" paper and their GPT-5.1 evaluation results. Redwood leads with their AI Control ICML paper and the Alignment Faking paper. This tells any visitor — funder, researcher, or journalist — within seconds what the lab's most significant contributions are.[^13][^3]

**5. Social Proof / Credibility Signals**
Near the bottom of the homepage, major labs include:
- Logos of partner organizations (universities, other labs, government bodies)
- Notable press coverage headlines
- Number of researchers trained, papers published, evaluations conducted
- Quotes from respected figures in the field
METR shows logos of OpenAI, Anthropic, Google (models they've evaluated). CAIS quotes Director Dan Hendrycks and shows credentials. This section is what convinces a funder that the organization is legitimate before they even click "About."[^13][^3]

**6. Newsletter / Email Capture**
FLI has 70,000+ newsletter subscribers and lists this prominently. Every lab with a public communication function includes an email signup. For an AI safety lab, this grows your influence network and provides funders a measurable audience metric.[^9]

**7. Footer**
Standard footer elements: social links (LinkedIn, X/Twitter, GitHub), legal (Privacy Policy, Terms), contact email, nonprofit status + EIN number (critical for grant due diligence), and a brief mission restatement.

### Page 2: About / Mission

This is the page funders spend the most time on. It needs to answer six questions in order:

1. **Who are you?** — Organization name, type (nonprofit, PBC, LLC), founding date, location
2. **Why were you founded?** — The founding story and the problem that motivated the lab's creation
3. **What is your specific theory of change?** — How does your research specifically reduce AI risk? Funders explicitly look for "a clear and demonstrable path from research to reduced AI risk"[^16][^1]
4. **What makes you different?** — Your unique positioning relative to Anthropic, METR, Redwood, CAIS
5. **What is your governance structure?** — Board of Directors or Advisors with names and bios; funders specifically check for real, accountable governance (not vague "our trustees are committed professionals")[^1]
6. **What are your values?** — Many AI safety labs have explicit values pages covering: intellectual honesty, epistemic humility, independence from commercial AI companies, public benefit orientation

**For Dee's Lab specifically:** The founding story should connect your entrepreneurial background, your focus on underserved communities, and your AI safety mission. This distinctive origin narrative is one of your biggest differentiators in a field dominated by academics and ex-OpenAI researchers.

***

### Page 3: Research

This is the most visited page by researchers, press, and technically sophisticated funders. The standard structure:

**3a. Research Areas Overview**
A clear description of each focus area with a paragraph explaining the problem, the approach, and why it matters. Every lab organizes research into 3–5 named areas:

- METR: Broad Autonomous Capabilities | Evaluation Integrity | Governance Advice[^13]
- Redwood: AI Control | Evaluations and Demonstrations | Applied Consulting[^3]
- Anthropic Research: Alignment | Interpretability | Societal Impacts | Economic Research | Frontier Red Team[^15]
- Apollo: Science of Scheming | Governance & Policy | Products (Watcher)[^14]
- CAIS: Technical Research | Conceptual Research | Field Building[^3]

**3b. Highlighted/Featured Projects**
A project-level view with a short case study format: problem statement, approach taken, what was found, and why it matters. Redwood's "AI Control" and "Alignment Faking" sections are excellent examples — each one explains the research in plain language with links to the full paper.[^3]

**3c. Research Philosophy or Methodology Statement**
Several labs include a brief statement explaining *how* they approach research — their standards of evidence, their publication model (open access vs. embargoed), and their criteria for choosing research questions. METR explicitly states it "does not accept compensation" for evaluations and explains why independence matters. This is a strong credibility signal to funders and potential collaborators.[^13]

***

### Page 4: Publications

A filterable, searchable list of all papers, reports, and preprints. Standard elements:

- Filter by: year, research area/topic, publication type (paper, technical report, evaluation report, blog post)
- Each entry: paper title, authors, venue (arXiv ID or conference name), abstract (expandable), PDF link, code/dataset link if applicable
- Sorted: most recent first by default
- Key convention: all major independent labs link directly to arXiv or open-access PDFs — no paywalls

METR's publications page is a gold standard: every evaluation report has a structured page with: model evaluated, date, partnership type (with company or independent), key findings, and download link. The transparency about whether the AI company was involved or not is a deliberate credibility signal.[^13]

**For Dee's Lab:** Even before you have publications, create a "Research Agenda" or "Working Papers" section that signals what you're working on and what your research questions are. Funders want to see intellectual seriousness, not just retrospective lists.

***

### Page 5: Team / People

This page is the second most important for funder credibility after About. The standard structure:

**Leadership (Director / Executive Director / CEO)**
Full bio with: photo, credentials, research background, prior affiliations, publications, and contact email or LinkedIn. Note: technical AI safety funders pay close attention to the academic and ML background of leadership. If the Director does not have a PhD or technical ML background, this should be compensated by a strong Scientific Advisory Board.

**Research Staff**
Each researcher: photo, name, title, research focus, and links to personal website or Google Scholar. Labs with fewer than 10 researchers often include full bios; larger labs include brief role descriptions.

**Board of Directors** (for nonprofits) or **Advisors**
Names, titles, organizational affiliations. Funders specifically check this for: real names (not anonymized), diversity of expertise (technical, governance, domain-specific), and whether any advisors create conflicts of interest with commercial AI companies.[^1]

**For Dee's Lab:** Even as a startup lab, listing 2–3 credible Scientific Advisors — ideally from the AI safety or applied ML community — dramatically increases funder confidence. Consider reaching out through MATS alumni networks, CAIS fellows, or EA Funds connections.

***

### Page 6: Blog / Insights / Lab Notes

This page serves three purposes simultaneously: demonstrating active intellectual output, communicating research to non-technical audiences, and building SEO authority that makes the lab discoverable.[^6][^17]

**Standard content types:**
- **Research explainers:** Plain-language explanations of published or upcoming research (Redwood's blog posts on AI Control and reward-seekers are good examples)[^3]
- **Field commentary:** Perspectives on AI safety news, government policy developments, or other labs' publications
- **Lab updates:** New hires, new projects, conference appearances, grant announcements
- **Technical deep-dives:** More detailed methodological explainers for technical audiences

**Posting cadence:** Major labs post 1–4 times per month. A dormant blog (no posts in 6+ months) is a red flag for funders and signals organizational inactivity.[^2]

***

### Page 7: Evaluation Reports / Impact

For labs focused on AI evaluation (like METR) or field-building (like CAIS), a dedicated page for evaluation reports or impact evidence is essential. This page answers the question every funder asks: *"So what actually happened as a result of your work?"*

**METR's Evaluation Reports page** lists every model evaluated with date, partner involvement, and key findings. This is concrete, verifiable evidence of work done.[^13]

**CAIS's Impact page** quantifies: 200+ researchers supported, 1,000+ students trained through their ML safety course, compute cluster allocations.[^11]

**For Dee's Lab:** Even pre-launch, this page can present your "Research Agenda Impact Thesis" — what specific outcomes would validate that your research had impact? Being able to answer this in advance signals maturity of thought that impresses funders.[^16]

***

### Page 8: Careers / Join Us

This is one of the most critical pages because the AI safety field is severely talent-constrained. Labs that clearly communicate opportunities for researchers, fellows, and staff can attract top talent even without top-tier salaries.

**Standard elements:**
- Open positions (with clear role descriptions and application links)
- Fellowship programs (stipend amount, duration, eligibility)
- Research internships
- How to apply for compute resources (if offered, like CAIS's compute cluster)[^11]
- What the lab looks for in researchers (this doubles as a signal of your research culture and intellectual seriousness)

METR's About page includes a description of their research culture and what kinds of researchers thrive there — this helps self-select the right applicants.[^10]

***

### Page 9: Get Involved / Support / Donate

For nonprofit AI safety labs, this page serves multiple functions:

- **Donate CTA:** For CAIS, this is prominently featured with options for one-time and recurring gifts[^12]
- **Partnership inquiries:** A form or email for organizations wanting to collaborate or commission evaluations
- **Media / Press:** Contact information for journalists
- **Newsletter signup:** Prominent placement with subscriber count if impressive (FLI: 70,000+)[^9]
- **Volunteer or Pro Bono Expertise:** For labs open to contributed expertise

***

### Page 10: Governance / Transparency / Financials

This page is specifically required to win grants from institutional funders. According to funder research, this is the most common missing element from nonprofit research organization websites.[^2][^1]

**What institutional funders specifically check for:**
- Board of Directors listing with full names and roles (no anonymized governance)[^1]
- Annual Reports (current year; if 2+ years old, it raises red flags)[^2][^1]
- Financial Statements or 990 forms (for 501(c)(3) organizations, these are legally required and publicly available)[^18]
- Conflict of interest policies or disclosure statements
- Funding sources — at least a description of revenue mix (grants, donations, contracts)

**Pro tip:** METR explicitly states it does not accept compensation from AI companies for evaluation work. This independence statement is so important to their credibility that it appears on their homepage. Your lab should have an explicit funding independence policy and make it visible.[^13]

***

### Page 11: AI Risk Overview / Educational Resources (Optional but Recommended)

Several labs maintain an educational resource section for non-expert visitors — policymakers, press, and general public. CAIS has an "AI Risks Overview" section on their homepage, MIRI has "The Problem" and "The Briefing" pages explaining existential risk, and FLI has extensive educational content on AI, biotech, and nuclear risk.[^4][^9][^3]

For Dee's lab with a mission touching applied domains (healthcare, workforce, underserved communities), this section can be tailored to explain AI safety to domain practitioners who don't have a technical ML background. This expands your audience significantly and serves a genuine field-building function.

## Part III: What the Top Labs Do That Most Labs Miss

### 1. A Clear Theory of Change on the Homepage
The weakest websites in the AI safety space describe *what* they research without explaining *how* that research reduces risk. METR solves this by showing evaluation reports — you can see what was evaluated and what was found. Redwood solves it by linking directly from the research area description to the landmark paper that introduced the approach. Every page should answer: "So what? How does this make AI safer?"[^16]

### 2. Independence Signaling
Every top independent lab makes its independence from commercial AI companies explicit and prominent. METR: "METR does not accept compensation for this work." Redwood: explicitly nonprofit structure. CAIS: publishes all code and datasets free. Anthropic, as a PBC, publishes its Constitutional AI and Responsible Scaling Policy because transparency signals accountability. Funders in this space are sophisticated — they know that safety research funded by AI companies has conflicts of interest, and they value demonstrable independence.[^19][^3][^13]

### 3. Research Agenda Transparency (Even Pre-Publication)
Several labs publish their research agenda — not just completed work, but *what questions they're currently investigating*. This matters for recruiting collaborators, attracting aligned funders, and demonstrating intellectual leadership. METR describes current work on "monitorability evaluations" and "CoT controllability" — research in progress. This creates ongoing reasons for visitors to return.[^13]

### 4. Named Researchers, Not Just "Our Team"
Visitors distrust generic team pages. Every researcher, advisor, and board member should have a real name, photo, and credentials. The absence of real names is a major credibility red flag for funders.[^1]

### 5. Press and Media Mentions
METR's website includes a dated press section with 15+ media mentions. FLI references its newsletters and press releases. This signals that the lab is recognized by external validators (journalists, policymakers, other researchers) — what credibility researchers call "earned credibility".[^20][^9][^13]

### 6. Active Social Proof (GitHub, Google Scholar, arXiv)
Major labs link their researchers' profiles to Google Scholar, arXiv, GitHub, and personal academic websites. This allows funders and peer researchers to verify the intellectual credentials behind the work independently. It also provides SEO benefits, connecting your organization to the broader research ecosystem's citation network.[^17][^6]

## Part IV: The Funder-Specific Content Checklist

Based on research into what institutional funders (foundations, government bodies, and major philanthropists) check before initiating a grant conversation, here is the specific content required on your website to be grant-ready:[^2][^16][^1]

| Funder Check | What They Look For | Where It Should Live |
|---|---|---|
| **Organizational legitimacy** | Legal name, incorporation date, 501(c)(3) status + EIN | Footer + Transparency page |
| **Governance structure** | Named board members with bios and declared conflicts | About page or Governance page |
| **Mission specificity** | Clear theory of change, not just aspirational language | Homepage hero + About page |
| **Program evidence** | Measurable outcomes, not vague claims | Research / Impact page |
| **Financial transparency** | Annual report (current year), 990 link, funding sources | Transparency / Financials page |
| **Organizational capacity** | Staff credentials, headcount, technical capability | Team page |
| **Track record** | Publications, evaluations, partnerships, fellowships | Publications + Featured Research |
| **Independence** | Independence policy re: commercial AI funders | About page or Governance page |
| **Geographic scope** | Where you work and who you serve | About page |
| **Intellectual rigor** | Active publications, blog, citations | Publications + Blog |
| **Community standing** | Press mentions, partnerships, peer references | Homepage + Press section |
| **Contact accessibility** | Direct email, not just a form | Footer + Contact page |

## Part V: Design Principles for AI Safety Labs

### Visual Identity
The color palette across major AI safety labs tends toward one of two modes:
- **Dark/serious:** MIRI, METR (black, dark navy, white — signals technical seriousness and gravity)
- **Clean/accessible:** CAIS, FLI, Anthropic (white backgrounds, muted accents — signals openness and public communication)

Apollo Research uses a clean white layout with red/orange accent that signals urgency without sacrificing professionalism.[^14]

**Typography:** All major labs use clean sans-serif fonts (Inter, IBM Plex Sans, Roboto, or system fonts). Dense serif fonts are avoided — they read as academic/archaic rather than technically credible.

### Research website design best practices (from design literature):[^8][^6]
- **White space:** Dense content without breathing room signals disorganization. Major research labs use ample padding between sections.
- **Mobile-first:** Over 50% of initial site visits occur on mobile — even for B2B/institutional audiences. All top labs have fully responsive designs.
- **Page speed:** Slow-loading sites (>3 seconds) cause ~50% of visitors to leave. Research credibility is undermined by technical incompetence in the website itself.
- **Accessible design:** High-contrast text, alt text for images, keyboard-navigable menus. Accessibility is a values signal for organizations claiming to care about broad human welfare.

### What NOT to do
- Do not use stock AI imagery (robot hands, glowing circuits) — it undermines intellectual seriousness
- Do not have a "coming soon" page for your publications section — leave it out entirely until you have content
- Do not list advisors without their permission (it happens, and it damages credibility when advisors publicly disavow the association)
- Do not use a Wix or Squarespace template that looks obviously generic — the perception of low effort translates directly to perceived low organizational capability[^21]
- Do not have broken links, outdated copyright dates, or annual reports more than 2 years old[^2]

## Part VI: Platform Recommendations

| Platform | Best For | Cost | Used By |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Webflow** | Polished custom design, no code needed for updates | $23–$39/month | Many modern research orgs |
| **Framer** | Fast, modern design; good for small teams | $15–$30/month | Newer labs, startups |
| **Next.js + Vercel** | Maximum control, developer-driven | Near-free hosting | METR, technical labs |
| **WordPress** | Large content libraries, blogging, SEO | $5–$30/month | FLI, some larger orgs |
| **Ghost** | Newsletter + blog combo | $9–$25/month | Good for community-first labs |

For Dee's lab starting up, **Webflow or Framer** offers the best balance of professional appearance, update simplicity, and cost. Both integrate with Mailchimp/ConvertKit for newsletters and have built-in SEO tools.

## Part VII: The AI Safety Lab Website Launch Sequence

For a new lab, website content doesn't need to be complete at launch. The minimum viable website that is funder-ready is:

**Phase 1 — Credibility Foundation (Launch Day)**
1. Homepage with mission statement, problem framing, and 3 research pillars
2. About page with founding story, theory of change, and governance
3. Team page with founder/director bio and advisors
4. Research page with research agenda (even if pre-publication)
5. Contact page + newsletter signup
6. Footer with legal name, EIN, and 501(c)(3) status

**Phase 2 — Research Credibility (Months 2–6)**
7. First working paper or preprint on Publications page
8. First blog post / research explainer
9. Careers/Join Us page once you have openings or fellowship opportunities

**Phase 3 — Funder Readiness (Months 6–12)**
10. Annual Report (even a 2-page summary for first year)
11. Transparency/Governance page with financials
12. Impact/Evaluation Reports section
13. Press section with any media mentions

**Phase 4 — Community Building**
14. Resources / Educational Content section
15. Events page (workshops, conferences, webinars)
16. Alumni/Fellows section (if running fellowship programs)

***

## Concluding Analysis

The top AI safety research labs' websites share a common architecture rooted in three imperatives: **intellectual credibility** (rigorous publications, named researchers, clear research questions), **institutional transparency** (governance, financials, funding sources), and **clear theory of change** (not just what you do, but how it specifically reduces AI risk). The labs that perform best with funders are those where any visitor — a sophisticated grant officer at Coefficient Giving, a press officer at the BBC, or a PhD student considering a postdoc — can answer within 60 seconds: *What does this organization do? Why does it matter? Why should I trust them?*

For Dee's lab, the differentiation opportunity is clear: most current AI safety labs are operationally and aesthetically indistinguishable, with similar academic-heavy aesthetics, similar team compositions, and similar research focus areas concentrated at the technical frontier. A lab that communicates with the clarity of an entrepreneur-founder, brings the accessibility of a practitioner, and focuses on the applied safety domains (healthcare, workforce, underserved communities) that the rest of the field explicitly does not serve will stand out visually, strategically, and in every funder conversation.
