# Annual PRISM AI Safety Conference
## Timing, Format, Duration & Cost Strategy for Audacion AI Labs

***

## Executive Overview

Audacion AI Labs is well-positioned to launch an annual AI safety conference anchored to the PRISM Research Framework. The global AI safety conference ecosystem is maturing rapidly but remains underfunded in community-driven, citizen science-oriented events. The field's own practitioners have publicly called for more AI risk-themed events across every format tier. This report provides data-driven guidance on the optimal month, duration, format, cost, and competitive landscape so that the PRISM Annual Conference becomes the defining citizen science and behavioral AI safety event on the calendar.[^1]

***

## Part 1: When to Hold It — Best Month

### The Competitive Conference Calendar

Understanding when the major AI safety events occur is critical to avoiding scheduling conflicts and maximizing speaker and attendee availability. The primary annual landmarks:

| Event | Typical Dates | Relevance to PRISM |
|-------|--------------|-------------------|
| ICLR | Late April – Early May | AI safety workshops, high researcher traffic[^2][^3] |
| ICML | June – July | ML safety workshops, frontier model evaluations[^4] |
| AI Safety Summit (Gov't) | May (2026: Paris) | Government-led; creates demand for civil-society events[^5] |
| AI Risk Summit (CAIS) | November (DC) | Policy/existential risk focus[^6] |
| NeurIPS | December | Largest AI conference; 2026 in Sydney + satellites[^7] |
| FAR.AI Alignment Workshop | Tied to NeurIPS/ICLR | 2-day, invite-only; held directly before NeurIPS[^8] |
| Stanford CAIS Annual Meeting | September (1 day) | Academic annual review model[^9] |
| The AI Conference SF | September 29 – Oct 1 | Applied AI; 3 days, SF[^10] |
| ODSC AI West | October 27–29, Burlingame CA | Data science + AI; accessible format[^11] |

### The Optimal Month: **September**

September is the strongest month for an annual PRISM conference based on four converging factors:

**1. Calendar white space.** The major AI safety summits cluster in May (government), June–July (ICML), and November–December (NeurIPS, AI Risk Summit). September has virtually no major AI safety events, creating an open lane.[^2][^6][^7]

**2. Academic and researcher availability.** September avoids the end-of-semester crunch (November–December), summer fieldwork calendars (July–August), and the January/February submission season. The Stanford Center for AI Safety deliberately holds its annual meeting on the first Monday in September for exactly these reasons. Researchers who attend ICML in June/July are ready for a focused community event by late September.[^9]

**3. Proximity to NeurIPS pipeline.** A September PRISM conference would sit 10–12 weeks before NeurIPS (December). This positioning allows PRISM findings to generate pre-NeurIPS buzz, attract researchers refining their NeurIPS submissions, and gives the lab a natural "from PRISM to NeurIPS" research-to-publication pipeline.[^8]

**4. Los Angeles / California weather and logistics.** September in Los Angeles and the Bay Area offers optimal weather, lower hotel rates than summer, and full return of in-person professional activity post-summer. The AI Conference SF already validates the September 29–October 1 window.[^10]

**Recommended target dates:** Third or fourth week of September — specifically **September 22–24** or **September 23–25** (Tuesday–Thursday format to maximize attendance from traveling researchers who avoid Monday travel and Friday departures).

## Part 2: How Many Days

### What the Field Does

| Conference Format | Days | Attendance Range | Model |
|-------------------|------|-----------------|-------|
| FAR.AI Alignment Workshop | 2 days | ~80–150 (invite-only) | Deep technical, curated[^8] |
| Stanford CAIS Annual Meeting | 1 day | ~100–300 | Academic showcase[^9] |
| AI Safety Summit (UK/US Gov) | 2 days | ~100 (invite-only) | Policy/government[^12] |
| AI Risk Summit (SecurityWeek) | 2 days | ~200–400 | Security + risk[^13] |
| AISST/MAIA Weekend Workshops | 2.5 days (Fri–Sun) | ~50–100 | Student/researcher sprint[^14] |
| Online AI Safety Conference (EA model) | 1–2 days | 100s–1000s | Field-building[^1] |
| 3-Day Regional AI Safety Conference | 3 days | 200–500 | Deeper collaboration[^1] |
| The AI Conference SF | 3 days | 1,000+ | Applied AI, broader audience[^10] |
| NeurIPS | 7 days (main + workshops) | 10,000–16,000 | Mega-conference[^7] |

### Recommendation: **3 Days**

For PRISM's mission and stage of development, three days is the right structure for the following reasons:

- **Day 1 (Research Day):** Pillar research presentations, working paper previews, citizen science data reveals, and academic-style poster sessions. Target audience: researchers, academics, alignment community.
- **Day 2 (Platform + Ecosystem Day):** Live PRISM platform demos, contributor showcases, panel discussions on multi-agent safety/behavioral drift, industry partnerships, and the Fellowship awards ceremony. Target audience: practitioners, technologists, community contributors, media.
- **Day 3 (Policy and Public Day):** AI governance panels, public keynote(s), workshops for policymakers, press briefings, and interactive sessions for the general public and new fellows. Target audience: policymakers, journalists, the public, new PRISM recruits.

This three-day structure mirrors the most effective models in the field. The 3-day AI Safety conference blueprint from the EA Forum specifically identifies this as the format that enables "in-depth networking, advanced topics, and catalyzing deeper collaboration". The AI Conference SF uses the same three-day structure to successfully blend technical depth with public-facing impact.[^1][^10]

**Year 1 exception:** For a first-year event, a 2-day format (Research Day + Platform/Community Day) is a lower-risk, lower-cost entry point that can be expanded to 3 days in Year 2 once the logistics infrastructure and contributor base are larger.

## Part 3: How Much It Will Cost

### Cost Framework

Conference hosting costs in 2026 range from **$150,000 to over $3,000,000** depending on scale, scope, and geography. Post-pandemic inflation has pushed costs 18–25% above pre-2022 levels. The per-attendee cost range is **$500–$2,500**, with most quality mid-range events landing at **$800–$1,500 per attendee**.[^15][^16]

**The biggest cost drivers:**
- Venue and F&B: 50–60% of total conference budget[^17]
- Speakers and entertainment: 15–25%[^17]
- A/V and production: 10–15%[^17]
- Marketing and materials: 10–15%[^17]

### Budget Scenarios for PRISM Annual Conference

| Scenario | Attendees | Days | Per-Attendee Cost | Est. Total Budget |
|----------|-----------|------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Year 1 Pilot (lean) | 150–200 | 2 days | $500–$800 | $75,000–$160,000[^15] |
| Year 1 Standard | 200–300 | 2 days | $800–$1,200 | $160,000–$360,000[^16] |
| Year 2 Growth | 300–500 | 3 days | $800–$1,500 | $240,000–$750,000[^15][^16] |
| Year 3+ Flagship | 500–1,000 | 3 days | $800–$1,500 | $400,000–$1,500,000[^16] |

### LA vs. SF Venue Cost Comparison

Los Angeles has a meaningful cost advantage over San Francisco for venue and hotel costs, while still being a major media hub that serves the PR/narrative mission. For Year 1 and 2, hosting in **Los Angeles** — particularly at venues like USC's campus, UCLA's Conference Center, or the LA Grand Hotel Downtown — reduces venue costs by an estimated 20–30% compared to equivalent SF venues. Year 3+ can consider rotating to SF to access the tech/research density of the Bay Area corridor.[^16]

### Cost Reduction Strategies from the Field

The EA Forum AI Safety conference blueprint identifies several cost-saving approaches used by existing orgs:[^1]
- **Travel stipends selectively:** Only fund travel for speakers and fellowship awardees, not general attendees. This can cut 15–20% from budget.
- **University partnership:** Co-hosting with USC, UCLA, or Caltech dramatically reduces venue costs and adds academic credibility.
- **Hybrid/livestream:** Broadcasting Day 3 (public day) free online multiplies reach without proportional cost increase. Apart Research's hackathons demonstrate this model scales.[^1]
- **Sponsor the contributor showcases:** Industry partners pay to present alongside citizen science findings, turning the conference into a partial revenue event.
- **Registration tiers:** $0 for PRISM fellows (incentivizes contribution), $150–$350 for general public, $500–$1,500 for corporate delegates.[^18]

## Part 4: What Others Are Doing — The Competitive Landscape

### Government-Led Summits

The **AI Safety Summit** (2026: Paris, May 15–16) is the flagship government-convened event on frontier AI safety and governance commitments. It is invite-only, ~2 days, and focuses on intergovernmental commitments — not community-building. The **AI Risk Summit** (Washington, DC, November 17–18) is run by the Center for AI Safety and focuses on existential risks and frontier model evaluations.[^5][^6]

**What PRISM does differently:** These events are top-down and closed. PRISM's citizen science mission is explicitly bottom-up — one million Field Researchers generating behavioral observations that no government panel or frontier lab can replicate. This is a clear positioning gap.

### Research Lab Alignment Workshops

**FAR.AI's Alignment Workshop** is the gold standard for invite-only research convenings. Founded in 2023 in SF, it holds 2-day workshops just before NeurIPS and ICLR, drawing "top ML researchers from industry and academia". The 2024 Bay Area workshop featured Anca Dragan and covered threat models, interpretability, oversight, and governance. The 2025 pre-NeurIPS workshop was held in San Diego.[^8]

**What PRISM does differently:** FAR.AI workshops are invite-only, ~80 attendees, and exclusively technical. PRISM's multi-pillar structure — especially the Post-Deployment Behavior and Interaction Dynamics pillars — covers terrain that pure alignment workshops don't: longitudinal drift, human-AI trust calibration, and citizen-observed behavioral patterns at scale.

### Academic Annual Meetings

**Stanford Center for AI Safety** runs a 1-day annual meeting (8:30 AM–5:00 PM, September). The 2025 event featured a structured day of faculty talks, keynotes from Waymo and NVIDIA, poster lightning talks, a 2-hour poster session, and two major panels on AI safety challenges and global policy. Total attendance was in the 100–300 range.[^9]

**What PRISM does differently:** The Stanford model is excellent for showcasing internal research. PRISM's conference should adopt the Stanford Day 1 structure almost exactly for its Research Day — keynotes, pillar presentations, poster session — but then extend it with the Platform Day and Public Day that Stanford doesn't need to run.

### Community and Fellowship Convenings

**AISST/MAIA Weekend Workshops** (MIT/Stanford collaboration) run 2.5-day residential workshops each semester. Format: Friday evening through Sunday afternoon, with talks, small group discussions, and research sprints. Attendance: 50–100 students and professionals. These are the closest analog to what PRISM needs in the early stages, especially for its Fellowship.[^14]

**The AI Safety Events & Training Newsletter** (2026 Week 20) lists active events including the Global Challenges Project Oxford (3-day residential, June), SPAR Demo Day virtual showcase with 100+ projects, and the Hardware Assurance Programme (6-day technical, August). The field is running more events than ever but almost none of them serve a citizen science community.[^19]

### The Online Conference Model

Apart Research's hackathons are the primary multi-day online AI safety programming currently available. The EA Forum blueprint argues that a purpose-built AI Safety online conference would surpass hackathons by offering dedicated networking, broader non-technical audience access, and explicit field-building programming.[^1]

**PRISM's opportunity:** A hybrid PRISM conference — in-person anchor in LA for the core community, with Day 3 live-streamed globally — would instantly become the largest AI safety event specifically designed for citizen scientists and would cost significantly less than a fully in-person event.

## Part 5: PRISM Annual Conference Blueprint

### Recommended Model (Year 2 onward)

**Name:** *PRISM Annual Conference on Behavioral AI Safety*
**Month:** September (target: 3rd week)
**Days:** 3 days (Tuesday–Thursday)
**Location:** Los Angeles (Year 1–2), rotating to SF (Year 3+)
**Format:** In-person anchor + Day 3 hybrid livestream

### Suggested Day-by-Day Structure

| Day | Theme | Core Content | Target Audience |
|-----|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| Day 1 | Research Day | Pillar lead presentations, working paper previews, poster session, keynote on behavioral AI safety | Researchers, academics, alignment community |
| Day 2 | Platform & Community Day | PRISM platform demos, citizen science data reveal, Fellowship Tier awards, industry panels, networking dinner | Contributors, technologists, partners, press |
| Day 3 | Policy & Public Day | Governance panels, public keynote by Dee, policymaker workshops, media briefings, free livestream | Policymakers, press, general public, new fellows |

### Budget Target (Year 1 Pilot — 200 attendees, 2 days)

| Line Item | Estimated Cost |
|-----------|---------------|
| Venue (university partnership) | $15,000–$30,000 |
| A/V and production | $20,000–$40,000 |
| Catering / F&B | $25,000–$50,000 |
| Speaker travel and honoraria | $15,000–$25,000 |
| Marketing and outreach | $10,000–$20,000 |
| Event platform / registration tech | $5,000–$10,000 |
| Contingency (15%) | $13,500–$26,250 |
| **Total Estimated** | **$103,500–$201,250** |

Source benchmarks: per-attendee costs $500–$800 for lean entry-level conference, venue/F&B at 50-60% of budget.[^15][^17]

### Grant Integration Note

Conference costs are grantable under NSF SBIR Broader Impacts, most foundation grants (Cartier, Knight, Simons), and federal AI research grants that include community dissemination activities. Budget the PRISM Annual Conference as a line item in grant applications under "Community Engagement and Research Dissemination," not as a separate operational cost.

## Key Strategic Insights

**1. September is the optimal month** — it occupies the calendar white space between ICML (July) and NeurIPS (December), matches the Stanford CAIS model, and aligns with LA's peak conference season.[^10][^9]

**2. Start with 2 days, scale to 3** — Year 1 risk management argues for a focused 2-day pilot; the 3-day structure becomes the flagship model from Year 2 onward.[^1]

**3. Cost target is $100K–$200K for Year 1, $400K–$750K for Year 3** — budget at $800–$1,200 per attendee as the base planning figure.[^16][^15]

**4. PRISM fills a genuine gap** — every major existing AI safety event is either government-led (closed), invite-only research workshops (inaccessible), or mega-conferences (generic). No event serves citizen AI safety scientists. The PRISM Annual Conference would be the first.[^1]

**5. Hybrid Day 3 multiplies impact** — live-streaming the public day reaches the 1M contributor goal audience while keeping core conference costs manageable. The online model scales to hundreds or thousands of concurrent viewers at minimal marginal cost.[^1]
